25th February 2003 – Hamilton, New Zealand

We had hooked up with my friend Cameron (from a mountain bike trip in Peru) in Australia, but apart from that it had been Scotland when we had last interacted, in person, with anyone we knew. Now in our last days in NZ this started to change. After finishing our Abel Tasman hike we headed north once more, caught a repeat of an LOTR movie, completely loved the art deco in Hastings etc and went to visit Gary who was on sabbatical from the University of Zambia to University of Waikato (ne Hamilton). Gary is an old friend from the days of CompSci and stuck with the whole academia gig while I slogged on with corporate capitalism. Oh yeah, and it started to rain again…

21st February 2003 – Bark Bay Hut, Abel Tasman, New Zealand

As a Scotsman (Glasgow = 49″ of rain a year (one inch less than NYC)) and a resident of Seattle (37.5″ of rain a year, but let’s not talk about the clouds) I really shouldn’t be *that* annoyed that we had a week or two of rain-drenched adventure. After all New Zealand is a not disimilar temperate climate; it’s a long thin island in a big ocean full of clouds; it’s very *green* which means it must *rain* but after our awesome Kepler Track hike we paid our dues. Running from rain in the south glaciers we headed to Arthur’s Pass (Pop. 54) only to be flooded out again – our new tent was not a boat!!

But we carried on, with panache.

Day 229 finds us bunking in bunk beds, wet, after arriving late, on the very awesome Abel Tasman Track in NZ. It’s a strange country where it has a bundle of great hikes, it advertizes them and people (and tourists!) hike them. And yet they are not overcrowded or subject to lottery etc (I’m looking at you Mt. Rainier; Alpine Lakes et al). The advantages of a great ratio of population:awesomeness (the US has this as well but it’s so dang large that the ratio of local population:local awesomeness sometimes overwhelms the awesomeness). And yes, I use the word awesome a lot now.

But 229 days into the trip, as our last newsletter notes, we were starting to think of THE END. The clock was ticking. The USA was approaching. Our time in NZ, and Australia, and parts beyond, was starting to become very, very finite.

(On a side note, I have no recollection of “Puzzle World in Wanaka” and need to talk to Danika about that when she gets off an awful I99N snarl up)

Wetter than a Scottish summer. – 19th February 2003

(Being a re-post of a ten-year old Yahoo groups newsletter update)

[Boots-Off Internet, Nelson, New Zealand]

We’ve spent the last week hiking our butts off and getting completely soaked, though not at the same time. We’ve experienced our worst weather yet in New Zealand and discovered that our new tent is NOT waterproof from below when sitting in a field three inches deep in storm rain! The rain has put a little bit of a dint in our plans: hopes to take a helicopter ride and full-on glacier hike were ditched, and we took rather a large detour to a place called ‘Arthur’s Pass’ only to have to leave again due to the blasted rain ðŸ™‚ Fortunately, the rain has ceased for the time being, and we managed to do some fun stuff before and during!

Last week we went on a cruise and kayak in the Milford Sound fiord, we overnighted on the boat and the difference between the windy and rainy evening and the blue-sky morning was a wonder to see. We followed this by tackling our first more-than-one-day “Great Walk” in NZ – the Kepler Track. It took us four days (though we could have done it quicker) and was quite spectacular. There were fiords, alpine crossings, mountain peaks (Mt. Luxmore) and enchanting forests. We had to carry food and big packs, but stayed in excellent NZ huts so we didn’t have to carry the big tent! We befriended quite a few characters on the walk and even managed to watch some beach cricket. All in all, we thought it was such great fun that we’re going to do another Great Walk tomorrow. This time, we’re doing the Abel Tasman track which is mostly coastal and includes lots of beaches and some beach crossings at low tide only! We plan to do this walk a little faster – just two days, just as well as our ferry leaves the South Island the same day we finish the track!

After the beauty of the Fiordland area we drove up the west coast in some damp and wild weather. Although this put paid to our planned ‘treat’ of a helicopter hike on the Franz Josef or Fox glaciers we did go for a guided hike on the Franz Josef glacier anyway. This was good fun, despite a little rain (snow would have looked nicer) and was not near as cold as you may have thought! That night was when the storm rain started that plagued us for the next few days, but last night we finally spent a dry night in the tent and woke up to sunshine and clear skies.

We’re chilling in Nelson – fully drying out from recent days and catching up on a few chores. Saturday night, we return to the North Island for a last few days before we head back to Australia. We’re considering this the start of the ‘journey home’, after a weekend in Australia we start the several-day trek to Buffalo where we’ll consider the main part of the trip over. We’re happy and sad to be considering our return – we’re not really done travelling but unless we want to do a lot of fruit-picking, our time and budget is up!

Here’s to being in Buffalo in two weeks!

Danika & Gordon

“Ben Lomond is my nemesis” – 10th January 2003

(Being a re-post of a ten-year old Yahoo groups newsletter update)

[Internet Laundromat, Te Anau, New Zealand]

“Ben Lomond is my nemesis” was what Danika was feeling the day before yesterday as we hiked up the Ben Lomond in Queenstown. This mountain is higher, steeper and more difficult than the Ben Lomond that we hiked in Scotland a few months ago. But we made it to the top despite that!

We’re now in the South Island of New Zealand. Since we emailed last we’ve hiked the fabulous day walk “Tongariro Crossing” (Mordor) which was excellent, driven down to Wellington and taken the ferry across the Cook Strait (accompanied by a few shy dolphins) to the South Island.

We’ve found that the South Island is bigger and more remote than we expected – we seem to have spent a lot of time driving about. We’ve been to the low-key and cute ‘cities’ of Christchurch and Dunedin (both far too cold to consider a permanent base) and driven to the picturesque Mt Cook region where we did a little hiking.

After that we headed towards Queenstown, the Adventure and Adrenalin Capital of New Zealand. On the way to Queenstown we stopped off in Alexandra in Central Otago where Gordon went mountain biking. It was good fun (he skipped most of the extreme stuff) and was very similar to East Washington or North California – strange to be so far away but so similar! After going to Queenstown Danika jumped off a bridge with a little bit of elastic attached to her ankles.

Yup!!! Danika Bungy-jumped from Kawarau Bridge!!! It’s totally incredible and she still can’t quite believe she did it – but if you’re going to do it, you might as well do it in Queenstown.

Now we’re down in beautiful Fiordland hanging out in Te Anau. In the next week we’ve got an overnight trip on Milford Sound and a large 4-day hike in the Kepler mountains to look forward to. Then we start to work away back up New Zealand on the long trek home.

Hope all is well with everyone,

Gordon and Danika

4th February 2003 – Dunedin, New Zealand

Nothing quite like a 4am foghorn to get you ready for Mt Doom, wow.

The hike was quite wonderful, barren and sunny and very, very windy and this was a good introduction to the next few weeks where we do a lot of outside activities including a certain “adrenaline-junky” craziness for one girl.

But before that we start to head south, heading first for Wellington (where we see a massive Gollum at the movie theatre), and then cross the water to the South Island and start driving around.

We rattled through a few places that in retrospect, while not metropolises (sp?) or major centres, we should have spent more than a day at (Christchurch, Mt Cook, Dunedin). We had a great time, espcially with our new tent, which was a three man one and kept out insects etc (the previous tent had cost $15 or something at the Australian equivalent of Walmart, and while fine in the heat of Oz had suffered in NZ with being incredibly small and porous for the horrible biting insects of the North).

For those who didn’t know, Christchurch – a charming old town – was mostly destroyed a couple of years ago in a big earthquake. The wikipedia entry contains the phrase “liquefaction affected the eastern suburbs” – http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2011_Christchurch_earthquake. So I guess the whole “earthquake mud slide” warning horn 4am alarm call wasn’t such paranoia after all.

And ten years ago tonight we stayed in Dunedin, which (along with a lot of NZ and particularly this area) is somewhat Scottish, being named after Edinburgh in Gaelic.

Next stop, Mordor… – 29th January 2003

(Being a re-post of a ten-year old Yahoo groups newsletter update)

[Internet Outpost, Rotorua, New Zealand]

Hi all,

It’s been a while since our last email, so be prepared for a long one below. We’re in New Zealand now, and have stepped up our pace to squeeze every last ounce of fun out of our trip. The aura of doom and gloom has started as we’re on our last leg, and the flights are booked back to the US – Buffalo on the 5th of March!!! But we’re not letting that get us down too much, because we’re in one of the best places in the world!

We finally got to meet Jane and Andy in Australia and had a great time. We were very cultured and went to see an arty play in a trendy gay neighbourhood and also went to see the symphony in a Sydney park (along with strawberries, champagne, fireworks and 80,000 other people). Andy and Jane are quite a hyper couple though, so we also got an express tour of the North Beaches of Sydney (including sand castle competition), hiked to Ballenjoey Lighthouse, swam, snorkelled, hiked around a national park (amidst another bush fire), went to the movies and had a few drinks. NZ seems like a rest after that weekend ðŸ™‚ We’re totally looking forward to another weekend with these guys when we finish with New Zealand.

For our New Zealand trip we’re repeating some of the car-camping and hiking adventures that we had in America. We have a cheap nasty (Nissan Sunny) hire car for the whole time here and we will be camping most of the time. We’ve actually bought a new super large posh luxury tent to stay in as the little tent we bought in Australia was a little too cramped and also let the small NZ mosquitos in!!!! We love our new tent, although Gordon is quaking in dread at the thought he may have to carry it (4.5 kg = 10 lbs) if we go for some longer camp-hikes.

Our adventures in NZ so far have included: mussels and chips in Auckland, a trip to the Bay of Islands in the Northlands, an activity overnight on “The Rock” (http://www.rocktheboat.co.nz/) with shooting, fishing, kayaking, hiking, snorkelling, musselfishing, jumping off boats etc.), a crazy evening night drive to Cape Reigna lighthouse for sunset, a drive along Ninety Mile Beach (http://goaustralia.about.com/library/weekly/aa041801a.htm), an evening digging holes in the sand at Hot Water beach, and a hike up the Pinnacles in the Coromandel Peninsula. We’re actually kinda tired and achy today (especially after yesterday’s strenuous hike) so we’re taking it easy and driving down towards Wellington for our ferry to the South Island on Saturday evening. En-route we’re going to stop and do one of the New Zealand “Great Walks” – the Tongariro Crossing. This is a hike across a volcanic plain with geysers, craters, mountaintops etc. It was also used as Mordor in a certain geeky movie made in New Zealand.

Ok, ok. So here is the lowdown on the whole “They made the Lord Of The Rings in New Zealand”-gig. They did. We’ve bought a guidebook which maps the locations to the movie and have started to tick them off. Yesterday we drove past Hobbiton and the Shire and tomorrow we go to Mordor. Around Wellington there are many locations that we’ll look at when we come back from the South Island, and in the South Island there are a lot of the ‘big’ locations & the Misty Mountains, Isengard, Fangorn etc. All geek heaven. And it is really pretty, and very varied. One minute you are in rural England, the next the Sahara, and then it looks like the Highlands and (soon) the Alps. We’re loving it – the only minor problem being that we’re back in Seattle/Scotland weather zones. Four seasons in one day – in the summer!

Gotta go find some orcs.

Cheers,

Gordon and Danika

29th January 2003 – Rotorua, New Zealand

Another interlude with 2013 work traveling means there is a big backlog of decade old adventure travel to recap on here. And by big I mean Australia. A small part of it, but it was BIG!

I saw the platypus ripple at Kangaroo Island (shades of Maturin in The Nutmeg of Consolation limited to no poisonous stab) and then we chased widlfires to Sydney where we stayed once again at Chez Andy & Jane, this time with the hosts in glorious attendance (Hello Danika, fiance person).

We then headed to NZ, which apart from being wet/temperate/kinda like home, was awesome. All I can say is, how often can you say “tomorrow we visit Mordor”. And the coolest thing is, 9 years and 50 weeks later Cory and Laura did too – Mt Doom. The whole UK/Scotland/deja vu thing happens in the South Island (if I remember correctly – no peeking) but that’s after we climb one of the windiest hills we’ve ever done (I’m looking at you Tongariro National Park).
