Garfield – minus Garfield

I loved Garfield in my late teens/twenties. And still have a softspot for it as a cartoon – right there with Calvin and Hobbes (and now being a father, I dread the concept of Calvin as much as I adore the idea of an imagination such as portrayed in that strip).

But who knew that Garfield was making such a deep statement on life on the sly? The blog Garfield minus Garfield shows strips with Garfield removed. The result is a portrayal of Jon, and life, as a disturbing unsettling place. As the blog intro says (with my spelling corrections):

Who would have guessed that when you remove Garfield from the Garfield comic strips, the result is an even better comic about schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and the empty desperation of modern life?
Friends, meet Jon Arbuckle. LetÂ’s laugh and learn with him on a journey deep into the tortured mind of an isolated young everyman as he fights a losing battle against loneliness and methamphetamine addiction in a quiet American suburb.

Surreal is the word that leaps to my mind.

Bang goes that idea

It was never top of my list of options, but “quitting my job and becoming a bike messenger” was definitely high above “quitting my job and working at MacDonalds”, and certainly on some long dark days while biking home from work it seemed more appealing than many other office-bound lifestyles.

However, it appears this is not to be. As the article “The plea of a dying breed: Don’t kill the bike messengers” notes:

Ten years ago, an estimated 150 messengers worked in Seattle. Today, the number is 50 to 75. Other cities have experienced similar declines, with The Economist reporting that New York has lost more than 1,000 messengers since the dawn of the new millennium.

Bang goes that idea.

Christmas Quarantine

Christmas Eve: “Well I guess we’d better buy a turkey, or pizza.”

It’s now official. The pede has applied bilateral sanctions on little L and her battle with The-Virus-That-Attacked-Christmas.

Our increasing list of missed engagements can now be projected into the future rather than being last minute cancellations. So evening drink soiree, Christmas brunch and Christmas dinner now join Saturday brunch, skiing and Christmas lights on the casualty list.

Technically D & I can still circulate within the social world, but we’re alternatively sleep-deprived, worried, crotchedy and occasionally vomit-covered (sometimes all at once too): we’ll probably keep such excursions limited to the minimum. Such as trips to the store for chocolate, alcohol and drugs filling prescriptions.

Christmas traditions start in the strangest ways, and in some sense I’m pretty resilient to the ongoing challenges – it is our first Christmas Eve all in our little house in front of the tree after all, so who knows what annual activities this will be the genesis of.

Merry Christmas everyone!

The W’s.

p.s. I may even catch up on some blogposts – I feel the need to bore you with the latest list of movies and books, because some of them should be avoided.
